About My Journey

How a six-year-old looking at a library book about Saturn turned into a lifelong obsession with the cosmos.

My Story

A Mind Pointed
at the Stars

It started with a dog-eared library book about the solar system. I was six, and the image of Saturn's rings made me feel dizzy in the best possible way. How could something so enormous and beautiful just float there?

Since then, every major leap in space science has felt personal. The Hubble Deep Field photo? I printed it out and put it on my wall. The detection of gravitational waves? I stayed up until 2am reading the paper. The first image from James Webb? I cried a little, genuinely.

Space is the discipline where physics, philosophy, art, and raw human ambition all collide. There's nowhere else you get that combination.

Favorites

What I Love About Space

  • James Webb Telescope — Images from 400 million years after the Big Bang. It's literally time travel.
  • The Drake Equation — A beautiful attempt to estimate how many civilizations might exist. The uncertainty IS the point.
  • Neutron Stars — Objects so dense a teaspoon weighs a billion tons. Physics has a dark sense of humour.
  • The Pale Blue Dot — Earth photographed from 6 billion km away. A tiny pixel in a sunbeam. Instant humility.
  • Binary Star Systems — Two suns dancing around each other like they've been doing it for a billion years (they have).
  • Black Hole Event Horizons — The point of no return. Even the vocabulary is dramatic.
Journey

My Space Milestones

Key moments that deepened my love for the cosmos.

Age 6

The Saturn Revelation

Saw Saturn's rings in a library book. Couldn't sleep for two days.

2011

Watched the Last Shuttle Launch

STS-135 live — the roar of the solid rocket boosters and the perfectly shaped exhaust cloud.

2016

Gravitational Waves Detected

LIGO detected ripples from two merging black holes. I remember exactly where I was.

2019

First Image of a Black Hole

The Event Horizon Telescope released the first photo of M87*. A fuzzy orange donut changed science forever.

2022

James Webb First Images

The deepest infrared image of the universe ever taken. It's been my wallpaper ever since.
